I worked with my colleague, Molly Kelley, to present research on questions
related to the trend of plagiarism committed by international students. The
research centered on the argument that it is a mistake for Western academics to
expect international students to fully understand our standards of academic
honesty without explicit training. We outlined a few techniques for developing
this awareness and linguistic techniques for avoiding plagiarism in tandem to
give students the tools they need to overcome this hurdle. We wrote a
[paper](../publications/2012-10-20-plagiarism) that was published in the
conference proceedings the following year.

During the presentation, we discussed research from Volkov et al. (2011) and
Landau et al. (2002) showing that students' improved understanding of
plagiarism results in fewer incidents of accidental plagiarism. This research
motivates two different activities which deal with plagiarism identification
and prevention. 

Our first activity focused on plagiarism identification where students are
given a reading from an academic journal along with a selection of citation
examples. These examples will range from the most blatant to the more subtle
forms of plagiarism. Students then discuss which cases constitute plagiarism
and which ones are acceptable. The instructor will lead the discussion
comparing the levels of plagiarism in the given examples and by demonstrating
how to correct the problematic ones.

The second activity focused on plagiarism prevention. For this activity,
students are instructed to take notes while listening to a lecture. Students
are overtly taught good note taking strategies such as using quotation marks
around direct quotes in their notes and using shorthand and summary to take
notes efficiently. When the students have finished the lecture, they are
instructed to write a paraphrase of the lecture using their notes to aid them.
Having completed he first activity, the students have an awareness of subtle
forms of plagiarism and are able to consciously avoid this form of writing.
This includes mosaic plagiarism in which students patch together improperly
cited phrases from the source material.

The presentation will end with an explanation of how to implement these ideas
and activities in a variety of classes for international students. Instructors
in attendance left with two sample activities that are easily adaptable to many
different subject areas.
