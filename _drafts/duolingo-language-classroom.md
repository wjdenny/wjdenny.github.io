Duolingo is useful in language classes because it gives students opportunity to practice their skills independently of the classroom environment, but still in a controlled/leveled situation with plenty of explanation. However, to get the most out of this technology, we need to make sure students are learning not just their target language, but how to learn a language in general. My goal as an (English) language instructor is to train my students to be better at language learning than I am. In reality, that’s the best any teacher can do.

I’ll defer the how-tos of the technology to Duolingo’s capable tutorials, and instead focus on the pedagogical aspects.

First, students need to SLOW DOWN.  While students may differ in their attitudes towards this type of learning, the one to really watch out for is students who try to do the entire course as fast as possible. This shows that the student is not aware of how language acquisition takes place. Granted, experts are still debating this, but it is widely acknowledged that breezing through a textbook, or any course, is not good practice. Practice alone does not lead to mastery. Instead, we need a mindful practice and a basic understanding of the better techniques in language acquisition, harvested from literature in cognitive science, behavioral neuroscience, cognitive psychology, applied linguistics, generative linguistics, psycholinguistics, or really any of those nebulous coordinations between linguistics, neuroscience, and psychology and their various subfields.

https://www.psychologytoday.com/blog/the-athletes-way/201305/can-practice-alone-create-mastery

That students can better manage their fear of mistakes is one benefit to using technology. But you can achieve this effect with nearly any homework assignment.

Go through the effort of teaching all the features of Duolingo and how they are relevant to students. The strength bars, streaks, etc.

Don’t let students shy away from speaking. Duolingo does give students the option of turning off speaking tasks, which is useful if they are using a device that does not have a microphone. As of this writing, there is no way for teachers to see if students are doing speaking tasks or not (though, many teachers have requested this feature and we hope to see some new development soon)
